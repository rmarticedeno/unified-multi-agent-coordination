\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{chap:conclusion}

The thesis proposes a protocol-adaptable boundary between linguistic proposal and executable multi-agent coordination. RQ1 is answered at the design and prototype level: canonical capabilities, typed constraints, task dependencies, authority, validation and completion contracts, artifacts, attempts, and persisted trace identity must be explicit to justify dispatch. RQ2 is answered architecturally: language interprets and proposes, while deterministic components resolve identity, evaluate enforceable predicates, authorize, refuse, and record. RQ3 is answered through bounded task-local auxiliary specifications whose method, schema, validator, read-only authority, and lifecycle are explicit.

RQ4 is answered by the complete clean v0.5 matrix. The symbolic boundary produced zero measured false acceptance, but the repaired hybrid achieved only 1.39\% feasible recall and 50.69\% accuracy. The direct LLM achieved 100\% feasible recall and 52.08\% accuracy while falsely accepting 345 infeasible repeated observations. The paired hybrid-minus-direct accuracy difference was $-1.39$ percentage points with a 95\% matched-pair bootstrap interval of $[-3.47,0]$. Thus RQ4 is supported only for fail-closed refusal; useful hybrid coordination and comparative superiority are unsupported. No independent adjudication or broad superiority is claimed.

RQ5 is answered negatively by the complete clean 3/5/7 campaign. The implementation supplies consensus-backed storage, generation-CAS configuration, resumable membership operations, fencing, linearizable authorization reads, and fail-closed quorum responses, but the measured campaign passed only 20 of 33 trials. Seven trials violated invariants, six ended in infrastructure errors, 19 of 104 executed checks failed, and 40 of 144 expected checks were not executed. Formation and several bounded cases succeeded, but crash-window, combined-fault, and some reconfiguration behavior did not support the stated safety and conditional-liveness claim. No claim is made for multi-host availability, Byzantine tolerance, automatic recovery after majority loss, or exactly-once external effects.

The principal scientific claim is therefore deliberately narrow. The implementation demonstrates how established symbolic, security, persistence, interoperability, and crash-consensus mechanisms can be integrated so that unsupported linguistic proposals are refused before action and authoritative mutations stop when quorum is unavailable. Duplicate external effects are suppressed only when receivers enforce both operation keys and monotonic fences. The work does not prove semantic truth, production safety, malicious-agent resistance, decentralized authorization, Byzantine tolerance, exactly-once effects, or universal protocol interoperability.

The technical evidence is complete for the negative claims reported and includes unsuccessful outcomes rather than selected reruns. It does not establish that an LLM is necessary once a typed request is available: the greedy symbolic baseline solved every designed v0.5 case, and both model arms required universal structural repair. Remaining release work is administrative: supply tutor and tribunal metadata, record the packaging commit, rebuild the signed-off PDF, tag, push, and archive the submission package. Longer-term research should begin from raw ambiguous language, improve small-model draft robustness, reduce repair dependence, add independent corpus adjudication, rerun infrastructure-failed fault scenarios, introduce mTLS with per-node identities and rotation, test adversarial registries and external domain agents, add semantic validators and replanning, place voters in independent failure domains, and evaluate snapshot and disaster-recovery procedures in broader deployments.
