\chapter{Discussion}
\label{chap:discussion}

\section{Interpretation of the Proposed Framework}

The framework reframes hybrid coordination as a boundary-management problem. The central question is not whether an LLM can produce a convincing plan, but whether the system possesses enough explicit evidence to authorize that plan. This distinction makes refusal a legitimate coordination result and prevents apparent linguistic competence from silently expanding the system's operational authority.

The proposed division of labor is asymmetric by design. LLM-based components interpret requests, compare semantically varied descriptions, and propose decompositions. Symbolic components decide predicates whose inputs and rules can be represented explicitly. This does not imply that all semantic quality is formally decidable. It instead confines deterministic claims to capability coverage, compatibility, dependencies, authority, contracts, and recorded evidence, while leaving open-ended quality subject to domain-specific validation or human review.

The local LLM reference runs reinforce this division. Both \texttt{qwen/qwen3-1.7b} and \texttt{google/gemma-4-e2b} produced schema-valid candidate outputs for all seven scenarios, but neither reproduced the exact symbolic requirement names used by the deterministic fixtures. The result is not surprising: local language models can provide useful semantic mediation, but exact capability identity is an implementation contract. The coordination layer must therefore normalize, validate, or refuse proposals rather than treating structured JSON from a model as an executable commitment.

\section{Relation to Prior Work}

The framework combines ideas that are usually treated separately. From classical multi-agent systems it adopts explicit capabilities, communicative acts, commitments, and protocol state. From symbolic AI it adopts structured planning conditions and verifiable constraints. From LLM-agent systems it adopts flexible language mediation and candidate decomposition. From interoperability protocols it adopts black-box participation through published interfaces. Its novelty claim is therefore integrative rather than a claim to have invented these individual mechanisms.

Unlike free-form multi-agent conversation, the framework requires a plan and its authorization evidence to exist outside the dialogue transcript. Unlike a purely symbolic coordinator, it accepts that user intent and capability descriptions may require semantic mediation. Unlike protocol-only interoperability, it treats discovery and transport as necessary but insufficient for trust and feasibility.

\section{Technical and Practical Implications}

The design provides a concrete accountability point: the coordinating role owns the accepted plan and its justification, while participating agents retain control over their execution. This can simplify auditing and failure diagnosis because planning infeasibility, authorization error, coordination failure, agent failure, and artifact-validation failure have distinct representations. The same concentration creates a bottleneck and a single point of failure when the role is implemented by one in-memory process. The proposed path is to keep one logical authorization role while using bounded retries, a durable coordination ledger, and hot standby recovery to tolerate crash failures.

Bounded auxiliary capabilities illustrate the framework's conservative stance. A missing transformation may be introduced only when its method, inputs, outputs, validator, authority, and lifecycle are explicit. This preserves flexibility for narrow language processing while rejecting the assumption that generated code or a general LLM can supply any missing expertise.

\section{Limitations}

The theoretical justification depends on honest and sufficiently precise capability descriptions. The framework does not establish agent identity, attest internal behavior, or defend against a malicious registry. It also assumes that a trusted coordinator applies the declared checks correctly. Distributed consensus, competing coordinators, and Byzantine behavior remain outside scope.

Contract validity is weaker than semantic truth. A schema-valid summary may still omit an important fact, and a formally admissible plan may still fail at runtime. High-impact domains therefore require domain validators, monitoring, redundancy, or human authorization beyond the generic framework. The current prototype includes a JSONL coordination ledger, bounded retry settings, stable session and attempt identifiers, and resume behavior that can skip already completed tasks. This improves crash-recovery evidence for simple sessions, but it is not yet a complete hot-standby design: lease management, coordinator restart orchestration, alternate-agent replanning after post-authorization loss, and duplicate-dispatch protection across independently restarted services remain future work. These mechanisms also do not solve malicious registries, dishonest agent cards, or Byzantine coordinator behavior.

The empirical evidence remains prototype-scale. The deterministic and Docker scenarios show that the implementation preserves the proposed boundary under curated conditions, but they do not cover adversarial registries, independently developed third-party A2A agents, physical actuators, or large benchmark distributions. The local LLM references characterize two small locally served models, not the broader class of LLMs. Claims about general accuracy, cost, robustness, or superiority over LLM-only and rule-only baselines therefore remain limited until a larger paired comparative study is executed.

\section{Threats to Validity}

Construct validity is threatened if task completion is treated as equivalent to justified coordination. The evaluation mitigates this risk by separating outcome, process, trace, communication-boundary, and coordination-ownership metrics. Internal validity is threatened by model nondeterminism, prompt sensitivity, local model loading state, and unequal baseline engineering. The batched local-model protocol reduces accidental model switching but does not remove prompt sensitivity. External validity is limited by the coordination-agent design, SDK adapters, A2A-backed implementation choices, chosen agents, and synthetic or curated scenarios. Conclusion validity is limited by the number and diversity of runs; larger paired studies should report variance, effect size, and category-level errors.
