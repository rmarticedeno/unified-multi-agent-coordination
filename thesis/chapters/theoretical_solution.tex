\chapter{Proposed Theoretical Framework}
\label{chap:theoretical-solution}

This chapter develops the theoretical framework proposed by the thesis. The framework addresses a specific coordination problem: how a distributed collection of heterogeneous agents can accept a problem stated in natural language, establish whether the problem lies within its current operational boundary, and construct a coordinated solution without allowing linguistic plausibility to substitute for executable justification. The proposed answer combines a central coordinating authority with distributed execution, capability-based admission, explicit commitments, and a symbolic feasibility decision. Language-oriented components interpret and propose; symbolic components authorize, record, and reject when the available evidence is insufficient.

The framework is intentionally independent of a particular communication protocol, model provider, programming language, or deployment platform. Chapter~\ref{chap:implementation-design} instantiates the concepts using concrete technologies, but those choices are not part of the theoretical claim. The present chapter instead defines the objects, boundaries, and properties that any conforming implementation must preserve.

The goal of the chapter is to state precisely what information and predicates separate a plausible proposal from an authorizable plan. It first identifies the purpose, assumptions, and design requirements of the framework. It then defines agents, capabilities, linguistic mediation, bounded auxiliary capabilities, commitments, communication properties, and feasibility. The subsequent sections state the framework properties, exercise them in a worked example, delimit the theory, and finally specify the safety and conditional-liveness obligations introduced when the one logical authorization role is replicated through consensus.

\section{Purpose and Contribution}

Open multi-agent systems derive value from heterogeneity. Agents can differ in ownership, internal architecture, reasoning method, data access, and operational authority while still contributing to a shared objective. The same heterogeneity also complicates coordination. A fluent description of a capability does not establish that the capability exists; a plausible decomposition does not establish that its subtasks can exchange compatible artifacts; and a successful delegation message does not establish that the delegated work has been completed. These distinctions are especially important when large language models participate in interpretation and planning, because linguistic confidence can conceal missing capabilities or violated constraints.

The framework treats this problem as one of \emph{controlled openness}. Participation is open in the sense that independently implemented agents can be admitted through externally visible contracts. It is controlled because admission, authority, capability coverage, and plan acceptance remain explicit decisions. The operational boundary is therefore neither the set of tasks an LLM can describe nor the set of outcomes that appear plausible in conversation. It is the set of task assignments that can be justified from the currently admitted agents, approved auxiliary capabilities, compatible data flows, and applicable authority constraints.

The principal contribution is a disciplined boundary between linguistic semantic selection and symbolic authorization. Natural-language reasoning is used where ambiguity and semantic comparison make it valuable: selecting admitted goal, trust-policy, artifact-contract, and exclusion identifiers from a finite catalog, or reporting unresolved terms. Once those semantics are admitted, deterministic components own dependency closure, typed-request construction, provider assignment, schemas, modalities, trust constraints, completion evidence, lifecycle rules, and authorization. The result of coordination is total at the planning boundary: either an accepted plan is produced with an auditable justification, or a structured infeasibility report identifies why no plan can presently be authorized.

Figure~\ref{fig:process-overview} summarizes this separation. The central coordinating authority is a logical decision role rather than a requirement that all execution occur in one process. Participating agents remain distributed and retain control of their internal operation. Centralization applies to the acceptance of the shared plan and the maintenance of its coordination state. A fault-tolerant implementation may therefore replicate coordinator processes or move state into durable storage, provided that each coordination session still has one authoritative plan state and one accepted authorization decision.

\section{Assumptions and Design Requirements}

The framework rests on five assumptions. First, participating agents are treated as black boxes with respect to their internal reasoning. An agent may use rules, a planner, a language model, retrieval, statistical learning, deterministic software, or a hybrid of these methods. Coordination depends only on the contract the agent exposes and on the evidence it returns. Second, the registry is evaluated as a session-level snapshot. Agents may join, leave, or change between sessions, but a feasibility decision is made against a definite set of available descriptions and states.

Third, capability descriptions and admission metadata are assumed to be sufficiently honest for contractual reasoning. This is not an assumption that agents are infallible. It means that the coordinator may use an admitted capability claim as evidence when constructing a plan. Misrepresentation, compromised identities, and adversarial capability advertisements require trust-establishment mechanisms beyond the present theory. Fourth, the central coordinator is trusted to apply the declared checks correctly. The framework constrains this role through explicit inputs, outputs, and traces, but it does not solve Byzantine coordination among competing coordinators. Fifth, deterministic validation is limited to properties that can be expressed in a contract. Schema validity, provenance, bounds, permissions, and required fields may be decidable even when the semantic quality of an open-ended result is not.

Within those assumptions, the design is governed by nine requirements. \emph{Openness} permits independently developed agents to enter or leave through explicit admission procedures. \emph{Vendor independence} prevents coordination semantics from depending on an agent's internal framework or model provider. \emph{Interoperability} requires shared representations for capabilities, tasks, status, artifacts, and errors. \emph{Distributed operation} keeps participating agents independently deployable and avoids shared hidden state as a prerequisite for cooperation. \emph{Coordinated authority} assigns responsibility for the global feasibility decision and the accepted plan. \emph{Simplicity} prefers direct delegation over decomposition and deterministic checks over discretionary linguistic judgments when both can decide the same condition. \emph{Observability} records decisions, commitments, execution events, and artifacts. \emph{Fault containment} treats timeouts, unavailable agents, invalid artifacts, and rejected plans as explicit states rather than exceptional conversational endings. Finally, \emph{bounded authority} prevents either the coordinator or a generated auxiliary capability from acquiring permissions merely because a request would benefit from them.

These requirements create a deliberate asymmetry. Execution is distributed, but authorization of the shared plan is centralized. The asymmetry is not asserted as a universal necessity for all multi-agent systems; decentralized negotiation and consensus are valid alternatives. It is a design choice for this framework because a single decision role provides a definite answer to the question the thesis studies: whether a requested solution is justified within the current system boundary.

The scope is correspondingly limited. The framework does not prove the correctness of an agent's internal reasoning, guarantee that an accepted plan will succeed at runtime, optimize resource allocation in competitive markets, or define decentralized negotiation among competing authorization authorities. A concrete implementation may use consensus to replicate the state of this one logical role without changing the theory. The framework also does not replace an agent communication language or transport protocol. Its claim is narrower: under the stated assumptions, it makes the basis for plan acceptance, delegation, and refusal explicit and testable.

\section{Conceptual Model of Agents and Capabilities}

The conceptual model separates an agent's implementation from its externally relevant coordination state. This permits a language-model agent, a symbolic planner, a database service, and a deterministic program to participate under the same abstraction without implying that their internal forms of autonomy are identical.

\begin{definition}[Agent]
\label{def:agent}
An agent is a tuple
\[
a = (\mathit{id}, C(a), M_{\mathrm{in}}(a), M_{\mathrm{out}}(a), \tau(a), \sigma(a)),
\]
where $\mathit{id}$ is a unique identity, $C(a)$ is a finite set of declared capabilities, $M_{\mathrm{in}}(a)$ and $M_{\mathrm{out}}(a)$ are accepted input and produced output modalities, $\tau(a)$ is an admitted trust or authority level drawn from a partial order, and $\sigma(a)$ is the agent's observable availability state for the current session.
\end{definition}

Identity and availability are included because capability alone is insufficient for delegation. An agent that advertises the correct transformation but is unavailable, unauthenticated, or unauthorized for the requested context cannot justify a feasible assignment. The trust value $\tau(a)$ is intentionally abstract: an implementation may derive it from credentials, local policy, organizational membership, or another admission mechanism.

\begin{definition}[Capability contract]
\label{def:capability}
A capability contract is a tuple
\[
c = (\mathit{name}, \Sigma_{\mathrm{in}}, \Sigma_{\mathrm{out}}, \gamma, e, \nu),
\]
where $\Sigma_{\mathrm{in}}$ and $\Sigma_{\mathrm{out}}$ are input and output schemas, $\gamma$ is a set of preconditions and authority constraints, $e$ specifies the evidence expected on completion, and $\nu$ is a deterministic contract validator for that evidence and the produced artifact.
\end{definition}

The validator $\nu$ establishes conformance to the declared contract, not universal semantic correctness. For a numerical service, it may recompute a result; for a document generator, it may only verify format, provenance, required sections, and declared bounds. This distinction prevents the theory from claiming that every open-ended capability has a complete oracle. Where semantic correctness is undecidable or expensive, the contract must state the evidence that can actually be checked and leave residual uncertainty visible.

Capability matching is represented by the relation $a \models q$, meaning that at least one capability in $C(a)$ covers requirement descriptor $q$ under the applicable schemas, modalities, and constraints. Coverage may be established by exact symbolic matching or proposed by semantic comparison and then confirmed against explicit contract fields. A linguistic similarity score alone is not sufficient to establish $a \models q$.

\begin{definition}[Registry and operational boundary]
\label{def:boundary}
Let $\mathcal{R}$ be the registry snapshot for a coordination session. Its capability space is
\[
K(\mathcal{R}) = \bigcup_{a \in \mathcal{R}} C(a),
\]
and its supported modalities are
\[
M(\mathcal{R}) = \bigcup_{a \in \mathcal{R}}
\left(M_{\mathrm{in}}(a) \cup M_{\mathrm{out}}(a)\right).
\]
The operational boundary $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{R})$ is the set of agent--capability pairs $(a,c)$ for which $a \in \mathcal{R}$, $\sigma(a)$ is available, $c \in C(a)$, and the admission and authority constraints applicable to $c$ are satisfied.
\end{definition}

The boundary is evaluated rather than presumed. It may contract when an agent becomes unavailable or loses authority, and it may expand when a new agent is admitted. Task-local auxiliary capabilities can extend it only under the bounded rules defined later in this chapter. The boundary therefore describes what the system can justify at a particular decision point, not what it might be able to learn or deploy in the future.

\section{Linguistic Agents and Symbolic Mediation}

A linguistic agent is an agent whose principal capabilities transform, interpret, or produce language-mediated artifacts. Typical capabilities include intent interpretation, summarization, classification, translation, information extraction, semantic matching, question answering, and natural-language explanation. Such agents are necessary because user goals and capability descriptions frequently arrive as prose, whereas coordination requires structured task and artifact contracts.

\begin{definition}[Linguistic agent]
\label{def:linguistic-agent}
An agent $a$ is linguistic with respect to a capability $c \in C(a)$ when the contract for $c$ accepts or produces natural language and the capability's primary function is to mediate between linguistic meaning and another linguistic or structured representation.
\end{definition}

This definition concerns function rather than implementation. A linguistic agent may use a large language model, a smaller statistical model, rules, retrieval, a controlled vocabulary, or a hybrid pipeline. Broad contextual interpretation may justify an LLM, whereas language detection, constrained extraction, or normalization may be implemented more simply. Treating every language operation as an LLM task would weaken the simplicity requirement and could increase cost, latency, and uncertainty without adding relevant capability.

The linguistic--symbolic bridge is consequently modeled as a sequence of artifacts and validation boundaries, not as a single translation component. The theory requires that language-mediated semantic selection, deterministic admission and compilation, symbolic authorization, and feedback grounding remain distinguishable even when a concrete implementation realizes them through the same software runtime. Four transitions define the core loop.

\begin{description}
  \item[Transition T1: semantic selection.] A user expression $u$ is transformed into a strict, non-executable semantic intent $\widehat{S}$ over identifiers admitted by an authoritative catalog. Every wire field is required; unresolved or ambiguous language remains explicit. A single repair may correct only JSON syntax or schema conformance.
  \item[Transition T2: admission and compilation.] Deterministic grounding validates $\widehat{S}$, applies explicit negations and exclusions, computes dependency closure, and constructs the typed request $P$ from authoritative trust, artifact, schema, modality, side-effect, and validation contracts. A bounded symbolic compiler then considers all compatible providers and constructs task identity, dependencies, execution order, artifacts, validation, and completion contracts.
  \item[Transition T3: authorization.] Symbolic predicates evaluate the compiled plan against capability coverage, data-flow compatibility, dependencies, availability, authority, fencing, exclusions, and evidence contracts. The result is an accepted plan $\Pi$ or an infeasibility report $I$.
  \item[Transition T4: feedback grounding.] Status updates, artifacts, timeouts, invalid outputs, and protocol errors become structured observations linked to the corresponding task and commitment. These observations may discharge a commitment, trigger bounded replanning, or terminate the plan with an execution failure.
\end{description}

Between T1 and T2, names are grounded only through the catalog's stable identifiers and declared aliases. Deterministic canonicalization preserves explicit prohibitions, applies declared defaults, removes redundant prerequisite goals already implied by a terminal goal, and refuses when a forbidden capability is required by dependency closure. Ambiguous or unresolved names remain interpretation evidence and cause refusal; they do not become hidden capabilities. Typed callers that already provide a valid $P$ bypass linguistic interpretation entirely.

Natural-language explanations may be rendered from either $\Pi$ or $I$, but the explanation is not the authoritative state. The symbolic artifact remains available for inspection and execution. Figure~\ref{fig:theoretical-bridge} shows the flow across the three conceptual lanes.

The framework is compatible with, but not equivalent to, classical BDI terminology. The admitted problem contributes to the coordinator's beliefs, a candidate plan represents a proposed course of action, and authorization establishes an intention-like commitment to execution \cite{rao1991modeling,rao1995bdi}. This is an architectural analogy rather than a claim that the tuples defined here constitute a complete BDI semantics. Similarly, structured delegations and status reports can carry communicative forces related to request, inform, and commit \cite{austin1962how,searle1969speech,cohen1990intention,fipa2002acl}, but their exact message encoding belongs to the implementation layer.

\section{Bounded Auxiliary Linguistic Capabilities}

An admitted registry will not contain every narrow language transformation that a decomposition may require. Rejecting an otherwise feasible plan because a trivial normalization or extraction step is absent can be disproportionate; assigning the same step to a general-purpose LLM can also be unnecessarily complex. The framework therefore permits a task-local auxiliary linguistic capability when the missing operation can be constructed from an approved, bounded method and validated against an explicit contract.

Auxiliary synthesis does not mean unrestricted generation of executable agents, arbitrary code, or newly trained models. It means constructing a temporary capability specification by selecting or composing methods from an admissible family and binding their parameters to the current task. The resulting logical agent may be realized by an existing sandbox, rule engine, model endpoint, or controlled execution service in the implementation. The theory requires the specification and its bounds; it does not prescribe the runtime that realizes them.

\begin{definition}[Auxiliary capability specification]
\label{def:auxiliary}
An auxiliary capability specification is a tuple
\[
x = (\mathit{purpose}, \Sigma_{\mathrm{in}}, \Sigma_{\mathrm{out}}, m, \nu, b, \lambda),
\]
where $m$ is a selected method from an approved family, $\nu$ is its deterministic contract validator, $b$ states resource and authority bounds, and $\lambda$ binds the specification to the lifecycle of one coordination plan.
\end{definition}

Four rules govern admission of an auxiliary specification.

\begin{auxrule}[Approved construction]
\label{rule:approved-construction}
The method $m$ must be selected from, or composed entirely from, an approved finite family whose operational semantics are known. Synthesis may bind parameters and connect approved steps; it may not introduce arbitrary executable code or an unreviewed model.
\end{auxrule}

\begin{auxrule}[Contract validation]
\label{rule:contract-validation}
The input schema, output schema, expected evidence, and deterministic validator $\nu$ must be available before the auxiliary capability is authorized. Failure of $\nu$ is an execution failure, not a linguistically negotiable result.
\end{auxrule}

\begin{auxrule}[Bounded effects and authority]
\label{rule:bounded-authority}
The capability must be side-effect free or restricted to effects explicitly authorized by $b$. It may not acquire persistent write access, external credentials, or privileges that are absent from the accepted plan.
\end{auxrule}

\begin{auxrule}[Lifecycle and provenance]
\label{rule:lifecycle}
The lifecycle token $\lambda$ limits the auxiliary capability to its plan, and every invocation records the selected method, parameters, inputs, outputs, and validation result. The specification does not enter the persistent registry automatically.
\end{auxrule}

These rules distinguish a bounded micro-capability from an attempt to synthesize expertise. Extracting fields from a fixed schema, normalizing a constrained identifier, or applying an approved classifier may be eligible. Producing a medical diagnosis, forecasting an unconstrained market, or generating privileged database operations is not eligible merely because a language model can describe a procedure. When no registered capability and no admissible auxiliary specification covers a requirement, the correct planning result is infeasibility.

\section{Coordination, Commitments, and Communication Abstraction}

The framework uses hierarchical coordination in a limited sense. The coordinator owns interpretation state, the registry snapshot, candidate-plan evaluation, and acceptance of the joint plan. Participating agents retain autonomy over whether and how they execute a delegated capability, subject to their advertised contracts. The hierarchy therefore establishes responsibility for the global decision without converting distributed agents into internal modules of the coordinator.

Central coordination is selected here because local views are insufficient to decide joint feasibility. An individual agent may know that it can perform one transformation without knowing whether another agent can consume its output or whether the complete artifact set satisfies the user's goal. The coordinator maintains the cross-agent dependency graph and the evidence required to justify the overall proposal. This choice introduces a concentration of trust and a potential availability bottleneck, which are acknowledged as limitations rather than presented as universal properties of open systems. Crash fault tolerance can be added without changing the theoretical role by replicating session state through a consensus-backed store and fencing ownership so another coordinator instance can resume after failure. Consensus here protects the state of one logical authority; it does not create negotiation among independent authorities. Byzantine coordination among mutually suspicious or competing coordinators remains outside this framework.

\begin{definition}[Commitment]
\label{def:commitment}
A commitment is a tuple
\[
\mu = (d, r, t, e, \kappa_t, s),
\]
where $d$ is the debtor agent, $r$ is the coordinating creditor acting on behalf of the requester, $t$ is the delegated task, $e$ is the expected completion evidence, $\kappa_t$ is the discharge condition, and $s$ is the observable commitment state.
\end{definition}

A commitment is created only after plan authorization and delegation acceptance. It is discharged when the expected artifact and evidence satisfy the task contract, or terminated with a recorded failure. Maintaining commitments as symbolic state addresses a weakness of conversation-only coordination: dialogue can indicate willingness, but it does not by itself establish who owes which artifact or what observation counts as completion \cite{singh1999commitments}.

\subsection{Required Communication Properties}
\label{sec:protocol}

The communication layer transports coordination artifacts but does not decide their meaning. To preserve the theory, an implementation must provide authenticated addressability, correlated task delegation, capability and contract conveyance, status and artifact delivery, timeout and cancellation semantics, and detectable failures. Messages associated with a session must carry enough identity and correlation information to reconstruct their causal order even when the underlying transport does not guarantee global ordering.

Capability descriptions must be versioned or otherwise bound to the registry snapshot used for feasibility. Delegations must identify the task, plan, expected artifact, and applicable lifecycle. Results must identify their producing agent and task so that validation and commitment discharge are unambiguous. Duplicate, delayed, or out-of-order messages must be detectable and handled idempotently at the coordination layer. Lack of progress must become an observable timeout rather than an assumption that the agent remains active.

These requirements define a communication abstraction, not a specific protocol. Any protocol or composition of protocols may instantiate it if the required artifacts and observations can be represented without exposing agent internals. The abstraction may be implemented with different transports, local runtimes, or linguistic runtimes, provided that each exposes the same capability, task, artifact, and trace contracts to the feasibility model. Planning, feasibility, and authorization remain coordination-layer responsibilities rather than transport-layer responsibilities. Substitutability is therefore conditional: changing the transport or runtime must not change the semantics of capability admission, task correlation, commitment state, or feasibility evidence.

\section{Feasibility Model}
\label{sec:feasibility-boundary}

Feasibility is a pre-execution judgment about whether a plan is justified by the current contracts and constraints. It is not a prediction that every agent will execute successfully. Runtime failure remains possible and is handled through commitment state and structured feedback. This separation is essential: without it, a failed execution could be mistaken for an unsound planning decision, or an optimistic planning decision could be defended by a later successful accident.

\begin{definition}[Problem request]
\label{def:problem-request}
A problem request is a tuple
\[
P = (g, \mathcal{Q}, \chi, \mathcal{O}, \Gamma),
\]
where $g$ is the goal, $\mathcal{Q}$ is a finite set of required capability descriptors, $\chi$ is contextual information, $\mathcal{O}$ is the required artifact set, and $\Gamma$ is the set of explicit constraints. A descriptor in $\mathcal{Q}$ may be marked as auxiliary-eligible, but eligibility is confirmed only by Rules~\ref{rule:approved-construction}--\ref{rule:lifecycle}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Coordination plan]
\label{def:coordination-plan}
A coordination plan is a tuple
\[
\Pi = (\mathcal{T}, q, \alpha, \prec, \mathcal{X}_{\Pi}, \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{out}}, \kappa),
\]
where $\mathcal{T}$ is a finite set of tasks, $q:\mathcal{T}\rightarrow\mathcal{Q}$ maps each task to its capability requirement, $\alpha$ assigns each task to a registered agent or an approved auxiliary specification, $\prec$ is a dependency relation, $\mathcal{X}_{\Pi}$ is the finite set of auxiliary specifications, $\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{out}}$ is the expected artifact set, and $\kappa$ is the plan completion criterion.
\end{definition}

For an auxiliary specification $x$, the notation $x \models q(t)$ means that its approved method and contract cover the requirement of task $t$. For a registered agent, coverage retains the meaning established earlier. The executor assignment is total when every task has exactly one accountable executor at authorization time; redundancy or collaborative subtasks can be represented explicitly as additional tasks rather than as an ambiguous assignment.

\begin{definition}[Feasibility]
\label{def:feasibility}
Given a request $P$ and registry snapshot $\mathcal{R}$, feasibility holds when there exists a plan $\Pi$ such that
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathsf{Feasible}(P,\mathcal{R}) \Longleftrightarrow \exists \Pi\;[\;&
\mathsf{Complete}(\Pi,P) \land
\mathsf{Covered}(\Pi,\mathcal{R}) \land
\mathsf{Compatible}(\Pi) \\
&{}\land \mathsf{Acyclic}(\prec) \land
\mathsf{Authorized}(\Pi,\mathcal{R}) \land
\mathsf{Verifiable}(\Pi)\;].
\end{aligned}
\]
\end{definition}

These predicates are operational acceptance criteria for implementation and evaluation, not a proof of the internal correctness of arbitrary agents. They specify which evidence must be present in the coordinator's trace before a plan may be authorized. The claim is therefore limited to pre-execution justification under the admitted contracts and registry snapshot.

The predicates make the acceptance criteria explicit. $\mathsf{Complete}$ requires that the task set and expected artifacts cover $\mathcal{Q}$ and $\mathcal{O}$. $\mathsf{Covered}$ requires
\[
\forall t \in \mathcal{T},\quad
\alpha(t) \models q(t),
\]
with every registered assignment drawn from $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{R})$ and justified by a recorded capability contract, and every auxiliary assignment drawn from $\mathcal{X}_{\Pi}$ after satisfying Rules~\ref{rule:approved-construction}--\ref{rule:lifecycle}. $\mathsf{Compatible}$ requires each dependency edge to connect recorded output and input schemas, modalities, and artifact identifiers, or an explicitly admitted bridge with its own validator. $\mathsf{Acyclic}$ requires $\prec$ to define a valid finite partial order. $\mathsf{Authorized}$ checks availability, trust, permissions, resource limits, and the constraints in $\Gamma$ against the registry snapshot and policy evidence used for the decision. $\mathsf{Verifiable}$ requires a completion-evidence contract and deterministic validator for every task and requires $\kappa$ to be decidable from recorded task states, artifacts, and validation results.

An infeasibility report is a structured artifact $I=(\mathit{code},U,E,H)$, where $U$ identifies unsatisfied requirements or constraints, $E$ records the checks that produced the verdict, and $H$ contains admissible remediation hints, such as admitting a missing capability or providing a required modality. A remediation hint is not a fabricated plan. It explains how the boundary would need to change before the request could be reconsidered.

The operational boundary is dynamic across sessions but fixed for a single authorization decision. If the registry changes during planning, the candidate must be re-evaluated against the new snapshot. If it changes after authorization, the affected commitment becomes unavailable or invalid and enters the feedback loop. This rule avoids accepting a plan from a capability view that no longer corresponds to the agents available for execution.

\section{Properties of the Framework}

The following properties state the behavior expected from a conforming implementation. They are conditional on the assumptions and contract descriptions introduced above; they are not claims about the internal correctness of arbitrary agents.

\begin{property}[Justified plan acceptance]
\label{prop:justified-acceptance}
If the coordinator accepts $\Pi$, every task has an accountable executor whose admitted contract covers its requirement, every dependency is compatible, and every explicit authority and validation condition used by Definition~\ref{def:feasibility} has passed. Acceptance does not imply that execution cannot subsequently fail.
\end{property}

\begin{property}[Simplicity preference]
\label{prop:simplicity}
If one admitted agent can satisfy the complete request under the same constraints and evidence requirements, the coordinator selects direct delegation before considering decomposition. Among evaluated multi-agent candidates, it prefers fewer tasks, fewer auxiliary specifications, and a shallower dependency graph, in that order, unless an explicit request constraint establishes another priority.
\end{property}

\begin{property}[Bounded auxiliary synthesis]
\label{prop:bounded-synthesis}
For every accepted plan, $\mathcal{X}_{\Pi}$ is finite; each member satisfies Rules~\ref{rule:approved-construction}--\ref{rule:lifecycle}; and no member remains valid after its lifecycle token expires.
\end{property}

\begin{property}[Traceability]
\label{prop:traceability}
Every decision can be reconstructed from the request representation, registry snapshot, candidate plan, predicate results, selected assignments, commitments, validation evidence, and execution observations. The trace distinguishes planning infeasibility, coordination failure, agent failure, and artifact-validation failure.
\end{property}

\begin{property}[Explicit refusal]
\label{prop:explicit-refusal}
At the planning boundary, the coordinator returns either an accepted plan or an infeasibility report. Missing coverage, authority, compatibility, or evidence cannot be replaced by an unsupported linguistic assertion of feasibility.
\end{property}

These properties provide evaluation targets rather than completed proofs. The implementation must operationalize each predicate, and the empirical evaluation must examine whether observed traces conform to the stated behavior.

\section{Worked Example}

Consider a registry snapshot containing three agents. Agent $a_1$ summarizes English documents and returns a textual summary with source references. Agent $a_2$ computes arithmetic expressions over typed numeric inputs and returns both a value and a reproducible calculation trace. Agent $a_3$ executes read-only aggregate queries expressed through a constrained query object and returns a tabular artifact. All three agents are available and admitted for the relevant data.

The user requests: ``Summarize the quarterly report, calculate the percentage change in revenue from Q1 to Q2, and list the five customers with the highest total order value.'' Interpretation produces a candidate request whose required capabilities are document summarization, extraction of two named quarterly values, percentage calculation, and constrained aggregation. The required artifacts are a summary, a percentage, and a customer table.

Direct delegation fails because no single agent covers the complete request. The summarization, arithmetic, and aggregation requirements are covered by $a_1$, $a_2$, and $a_3$, respectively. The registry lacks the narrow transformation that extracts the Q1 and Q2 revenue cells from a table with a known schema. This gap is auxiliary-eligible: an approved column-selection method can be parameterized with the two quarter labels, its output schema is a pair of currency values, and its validator checks column identity, numeric type, provenance, and cardinality. The coordinator therefore proposes the task-local specification $x_1$ rather than inventing a general table-understanding agent.

The candidate plan contains tasks $t_1$ for summarization, $t_2$ for percentage calculation, and $t_3$ for constrained aggregation, together with auxiliary specification $x_1$. The report is supplied independently to $t_1$ and $x_1$; the validated revenue pair produced by $x_1$ is the input to $t_2$; and a typed aggregate request is supplied to $t_3$. The outputs of $t_1$, $t_2$, and $t_3$ are collected into the required result. Figure~\ref{fig:worked-example} presents this plan as a task-and-artifact graph rather than as another architectural overview.

Feasibility holds because the task set covers the required capabilities and artifacts, each assignment is admitted, the extractor satisfies the auxiliary rules, the revenue-pair schema is compatible with the calculator input, the dependency graph is acyclic, and every task has a contract validator and completion condition. Authorization creates commitments for $a_1$, $a_2$, and $a_3$ and records the lifecycle of $x_1$.

Now extend the request with ``predict next year's revenue.'' No registered agent advertises a forecasting capability, and forecasting cannot be reduced to an approved bounded language transformation with deterministic contract validation. The coordinator therefore returns an infeasibility report identifying the uncovered forecasting requirement. It may state that admitting a forecasting agent would change the operational boundary, but it does not synthesize such expertise or allow the LLM's narrative prediction to stand in for the missing capability.

The example illustrates the intended division of labor. Linguistic interpretation identifies the requested operations and proposes a decomposition. Symbolic authorization verifies the exact capability and artifact chain. Auxiliary synthesis is used only for a narrow, controlled transformation. Explicit refusal preserves the boundary when the missing capability is substantive.

\section{Limitations and Chapter Synthesis}

The framework has several limitations. Its contractual reasoning depends on the quality and honesty of admitted descriptions. A malicious or substantially inaccurate capability claim can invalidate the justification even if the coordinator applies the predicates correctly. Trust establishment, attestation, reputation, and adversarial admission are therefore complementary research problems. The session-snapshot model also simplifies highly dynamic environments; frequent changes may require repeated authorization and could reduce practical liveness.

The coordinator is a logical concentration of authority and state. This simplifies accountability and makes the feasibility decision explicit, but it creates a bottleneck and a single point of failure if realized as one volatile process or one non-replicated database. The implementation mitigates this physical failure mode by replicating authoritative registry and session state through etcd/Raft and fencing session ownership. The request, registry snapshot, authorization decision, commitments, observations, artifacts, attempts, terminal result, and trace can therefore survive a coordinator crash while a majority of voters remains available. This is crash-fault tolerance, not Byzantine agreement; majority loss stops progress, and malicious coordinators, adversarial registries, and compromised consensus members remain outside the framework.

\section{Consensus-Backed Safety and Conditional Liveness}

Replication does not change the logical authorization model: it supplies one ordered authoritative history to multiple physical coordinator processes. The implementation therefore relies on two classes of property. Safety properties must hold in every measured execution, including when progress stops. Liveness properties are conditional on eventual communication, a surviving voter majority, functioning storage, and agents that eventually answer or time out. Raft ordering is inherited from the pinned etcd implementation; the application remains responsible for the state-machine predicates layered above that order.

No mutation is authorized from a stale or non-quorate snapshot, and no process may silently fall back from etcd to JSONL or PostgreSQL when etcd is configured as authoritative. Membership change is itself state: configuration updates carry an expected generation, and an add, promotion, or removal is represented by one durable operation whose phase can be reconciled against the actual etcd member list after coordinator failure.

Deterministic validation has a deliberately limited meaning. A validator can confirm contract conformance and available evidence; it cannot guarantee the truth, usefulness, or safety of every semantically open-ended artifact. Human review, redundant agents, domain-specific verification, or runtime monitoring may be required for high-impact tasks. Likewise, an accepted plan establishes pre-execution justification rather than guaranteed completion. Agents can fail, networks can partition, and external resources can change after authorization. The definitions and predicates are therefore an executable formalization of the proposed boundary, not a proof of global soundness, completeness, decidability limits, or computational complexity for arbitrary agent ecosystems.

Auxiliary capabilities are intentionally conservative. Restricting them to approved methods prevents the framework from treating code generation or model generation as an unbounded source of new competence, but it also means that some technically solvable requests will be rejected until a suitable method or agent is admitted. This is a purposeful tradeoff: the framework values a defensible boundary over maximal apparent coverage.

Within these limits, the contribution is a protocol-independent coordination model that combines distributed agents with central plan authorization, linguistic proposal, symbolic feasibility, explicit commitments, bounded auxiliary capabilities, and informative infeasibility. Chapter~\ref{chap:implementation-design} maps these abstractions to a concrete system design.
